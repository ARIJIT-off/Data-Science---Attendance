The journey of making this "ATTENDANCE - CSE (DATA SCIENCE)" was my toughest journey of building any website or application. It was a journey that started from zero — with very little knowledge of frontend development, basic HTML, basic CSS, and just like a grain of sand on a beach, a tiny bit of SQL and DBMS. I had participated in only one hackathon, won just one ideathon by a fluke, and after our 4th semester results were published, got a backlog in Maths — a subject in which I once used to call myself a genius. This is a rough picture of my mental state when I was building this project.

Okay, you might say that AI tools are already out there and they helped me — I won't claim I didn't use them. Of course I did. I used Antigravity, Claude Code, and Emergent. But you know what? A vibe coder who always chills out with a cup of black coffee, types useless prompts, and keeps copy-pasting bug-ridden code — codes nothing and cannot call himself a developer. I used Antigravity and Claude too, only to be greeted with: "You need at least 50 AI Credits to send messages. To continue using [platform], purchase more AI Credits. Your plan's baseline quota will refresh on [date & time]." Trust me, such messages are enough to demoralise you when you are doing two internships, preparing for the GATE exam, and just want to finish this side project before your semester break ends.

Here, I am not going to tell you which feature does what, what the tech stack is, and so on — those are things you will figure out just from 3–4 regular uses, and everything is already in my README file. I am here simply to document my sleepless nights: the World Cup match notifications pinging at odd hours, the amount of time I spent on YouTube. I still remember one night I watched 17 videos just to understand SMTP (Simple Mail Transfer Protocol) — literally going down a rabbit hole — and from there, tried to code a system whose only job was sending emails. From there came OTP generation, then the database, then the server. Weeks of sleepless nights, hundreds of YouTube videos, a boring vacation with no long drives or road trips — just a laptop, fingers constantly hitting the keyboard, and countless cups of black coffee. All of this helped me to build this whole gigantic system, brick by brick.

But okay — with a deep breath and an emotional monologue behind me — I need to tell you: "I am happy." Yes, I built the UI with the help of Antigravity. We always want to live in a home that is well-furnished and beautifully coloured — that is what AI does. It decorates your home, furnishes your rooms, colours your walls. But ultimately, you are the developer. You are the one who has to purchase the land and lay the bricks yourself. In the end, real users are going to use your creation — your dedication, your skills, and your journey will be what is questioned. No one is there to justify Antigravity's "Rate Limit Error." I just wanted to document myself. My almost 75 commits and 25 deployments in the Git repository will tell you my story — the journey of building the SMTP setup, then switching to Nodemailer, setting up mail sending, configuring app passwords, enabling access from anywhere via IP 0.0.0.0/0, deploying to Vercel and MongoDB with Mongoose, spending 3 weeks writing server.js, and then stripping out all those lines to migrate to Mongoose — just to be able to deploy on a serverless platform like Vercel.

Coding ultimately makes you a workaholic. You suddenly start to romanticise your boring keyboard and your unsolvable bugs. Every error keeps asking: "How far are you willing to go?"

I am not saying it is a war, nor is it poetry — but every error message is there to tell you that you are not worthy, that you won't survive, that the job market is evolving, that AI will replace you, blah blah blah.

But every tick mark in your Jupyter Notebook, every "Code executed successfully" message is there to encourage you. You start thinking about your LPAs, all the praise you are going to receive tomorrow — and now you want to tell your professors and friends, over and over again: "Look what I built. Look what it does."

Every time you code, you grow more expressive. This is the magic I felt while building this whole application. It left me mesmerised — how LLMs encouraged me more than my best friend ever did, how sunlight felt dimmer than the glow of my own desktop screen, how two missing semicolons made a 21-year-old cry. This is what 2 months of relentless coding and the desire to build something useful for someone can do to you.

I hope the magic of code and coders continues — but for now, I AM HAPPY.
